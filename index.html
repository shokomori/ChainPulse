How Verification Works

🔑

1. DID Resolution

The journalist's W3C Decentralized Identifier is resolved from the blockchain, retrieving their public cryptographic key.

#

2. Hash Comparison

The current article content is hashed with SHA-256 and compared against the immutable on-chain record registered at publication.

✍

3. Signature Validation

The cryptographic signature on the content hash is validated against the journalist's public key from their DID document.

✓

4. Result

A tamper-evident result is returned: Authentic, Tampered, or Pending — with full chain of custody from author to publication.

About
ChainPulse

A thesis project exploring the intersection of blockchain technology, decentralized identity, and the future of trustworthy digital journalism in the Philippines.

The Problem

The proliferation of misinformation, fabricated news, and algorithmically amplified false narratives has emerged as one of the most significant threats to democratic discourse in the twenty-first century. Across the Philippines, the rapid spread of unverified content through social media and online news outlets has eroded public trust in journalism and fueled political polarization.

Traditional content authentication mechanisms rely on centralized institutional trust — the reputation of news organizations and the editorial policies enforced by media companies. These are inherently vulnerable because they depend on centralized actors who can be pressured, compromised, or impersonated.

Our Solution

ChainPulse is a Web3-based news ecosystem that leverages blockchain technology and W3C Decentralized Identifiers (DID) to establish verifiable provenance, tamper-resistant content records, and cryptographically guaranteed attribution for news articles and journalistic content.

🔑

Decentralized Identity

Journalists register W3C-standard DIDs on the Ethereum blockchain, cryptographically anchoring their professional identity without relying on any central authority.

#

Content Hashing

Every article is hashed with SHA-256 and registered on-chain. Any subsequent alteration changes the hash, making tampering detectable with mathematical certainty.

📜

Smart Contract Governance

Solidity smart contracts automate the content registry, editorial workflow approvals, and amendment logging — providing a transparent, auditable governance layer.

📦

Decentralized Storage

Article content is stored on IPFS while only cryptographic hashes and metadata are recorded on-chain, balancing immutability with storage efficiency.

System Architecture Flow

1
Journalist registers DID and generates secp256k1 key pair
Identity anchored to IdentityRegistry.sol on Ethereum Sepolia Testnet. DID document stored on-chain with public key material.
2
Author writes article and computes SHA-256 content hash
Content is hashed deterministically. Any character change produces a completely different hash, making tampering immediately detectable.
3
Journalist signs content hash with private key
Cryptographic signature links the content to the journalist's verified identity, creating non-repudiable authorship attribution.
4
Smart contract validates signature and registers article
ContentRegistry.sol's registerArticle() function validates the signature against the journalist's on-chain DID and creates an immutable record.
5
Editorial workflow approval via EditorialWorkflow.sol
An editor counter-signs the article through the smart contract, adding a second layer of cryptographic attestation before publication.
6
Article published with blockchain anchor and IPFS storage
Full article stored on IPFS. On-chain record contains hash, author DID, editorial DID, timestamp, and IPFS CID for permanent reference.
7
Reader verifies in real time — no central authority required
Any reader or third-party auditor can verify article authenticity by comparing the current content hash against the immutable on-chain record.

Academic Context

This system is developed as a thesis project at the Holy Angel University School of Computing in Angeles City, Pampanga, Philippines. The research is submitted in partial fulfillment of the requirements for the degree of Bachelor of Science in Computer Science, March 2026.

The study employs the Evolutionary Prototyping Model as its software development methodology, with iterative refinement across multiple cycles based on participant evaluation outcomes from journalists, editors, and readers in the Philippine context.

All evaluation is conducted on an Ethereum Sepolia testnet using simulated journalist identities and fictitious news content, in full compliance with the Data Privacy Act of 2012 (Republic Act No. 10173) and the implementing rules of the National Privacy Commission.
